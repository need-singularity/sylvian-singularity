## Background and Context

### Unihemispheric Slow-Wave Sleep (USWS)

Dolphins and many other cetaceans never fully lose consciousness. Instead, they practice unihemispheric slow-wave sleep (USWS): one hemisphere enters slow-wave delta sleep while the other remains in active beta/gamma wakefulness. This allows:

- Continuous surface access for breathing
- One eye remains open (contralateral to awake hemisphere)
- Basic motor function preserved
- Approximately equal time in sleep alternates between hemispheres over a rest period

This is not a minor variation. The dolphin brain at any normal moment is running two largely independent processing streams with dramatically different activity levels.

### Tetrodotoxin (TTX) and Pufferfish Behavior

TTX is one of the most potent neurotoxins known. Its mechanism:
- Blocks voltage-gated Na+ channels (Nav) with extreme selectivity
- Prevents action potential generation and propagation
- At sub-lethal doses: reduces firing rates, slows conduction, decreases excitability
- At moderate doses: produces altered perception, numbness, sedation
- Dolphins have been filmed passing pufferfish in a circle and entering a floating, trance-like state afterward

The dolphin behavior is deliberate: careful handling of the pufferfish (to avoid killing it and losing the TTX source), passing in a social circle, and subsequent group trance states with apparent mirror-gazing and unusual self-directed behavior.

### Why This Is Not Like Any Human Drug Experience

Human brains sleep bilaterally. All known human drug states — including the most profound psychedelics, anesthetics, and dissociatives — affect both hemispheres simultaneously from the same baseline. There is no human analogue to entering a drug state from an asymmetric bilateral baseline where one hemisphere is already dramatically suppressed.

The TTX dolphin trance is therefore a qualitatively novel class of consciousness alteration.

## Limitations

1. **No direct dolphin TTX EEG data exists.** The entire electrophysiological prediction is untested. Ethical and practical constraints make this experiment extremely difficult.

2. **Na+ channel density estimates are extrapolated.** Direct measurements of per-hemisphere Nav density during USWS in dolphins have not been published. The quantitative estimates here are derived from general mammalian sleep physiology, not dolphin-specific data.

3. **TTX dose is unknown.** We do not know how much TTX dolphins actually absorb from handling pufferfish. The dose may be too small to produce the predicted effects, or the absorption route (mucous membranes?) may limit bioavailability.

4. **GZ-dependent mapping is speculative.** The cross-hemisphere tension calculation using G=D×P/I is a model interpretation. The model itself is unverified. Do not treat numerical tension values as established facts.

5. **Behavioral interpretation is circumstantial.** The floating, trance-like behavior and mirror-gazing have not been rigorously studied under controlled conditions with confirmed TTX involvement.

6. **Alternative explanations exist.** The TTX behavior could serve entirely different functions (social bonding without pharmacological specificity, learned behavior from observation, or accidental intoxication that became habituated).

7. **Corpus callosum anatomy in dolphins is variable.** The claim that the dolphin callosum is "proportionally smaller" requires species-specific anatomical data.
