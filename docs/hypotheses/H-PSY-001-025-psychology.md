# H-PSY-001 to H-PSY-025: Psychology / Emotion / Cognition Hypotheses

## Overview

> Do the fundamental constants of perfect number 6 (sigma=12, tau=4, phi=2),
> the Golden Zone [0.212, 0.5], and 1/e appear naturally in empirical
> psychology, emotion science, and cognitive science?

**Golden Zone dependency**: ALL hypotheses below are Golden-Zone-dependent (unverified model).
The psychology facts themselves are empirical, but the *mappings* to n=6 arithmetic are model-dependent.

**Verification method**: Each claim is checked for (1) arithmetic correctness,
(2) factual accuracy of the psychology claim, (3) Texas Sharpshooter p-value.

## A. Basic Emotions (H-PSY-001 to H-PSY-005)

### H-PSY-001: Ekman's 6 Basic Emotions = n = 6 (Perfect Number)

> Ekman (1972) identified exactly 6 universal basic emotions:
> happiness, sadness, anger, fear, surprise, disgust.
> Count = 6 = the perfect number itself.

**Psychology fact**: Ekman's original 1972 paper lists exactly 6. VERIFIED.
Some later work adds contempt (7th), but the canonical set is 6.

**n=6 mapping**: Direct — count of basic emotions = n itself.

**Grade**: This is a direct numerical coincidence. 6 is a small number that
appears in many contexts. The probability of any psychological taxonomy having
exactly 6 items is non-trivial (~1/10 for range 3-12).

```
  Ekman emotions:  {happiness, sadness, anger, fear, surprise, disgust}
  Count          = 6
  Perfect number = 6
  Match          = EXACT

  But: Ekman chose to split/lump categories — could have been 5 or 7.
  Later work (Ekman 1999) added contempt → 7.
  Cross-cultural studies suggest 4-6 universal categories.
```

**Grade: 🟧 — Exact match but likely coincidence (small number, post-hoc)**

### H-PSY-002: Plutchik's 8 Primary Emotions in 4 Opposite Pairs = tau(6) Pairs

> Plutchik's (1980) wheel of emotions has 8 primary emotions arranged in
> 4 opposite pairs: joy-sadness, trust-disgust, fear-anger, surprise-anticipation.
> Number of pairs = 4 = tau(6).

**Psychology fact**: Plutchik's wheel has exactly 8 primaries in 4 pairs. VERIFIED.

**n=6 mapping**: 4 pairs = tau(6) = number of divisors of 6 = |{1,2,3,6}| = 4.

```
  Plutchik pairs:
    1. joy       <-> sadness
    2. trust     <-> disgust
    3. fear      <-> anger
    4. surprise  <-> anticipation

  tau(6) = 4
  Match  = EXACT
```

**Concern**: tau(6)=4 is a very common small number. The number of pairs in any
8-item list is always 4 if you pair them all. The real claim is that there are
exactly 8 primaries, and 8/2=4=tau(6). This is weak.

**Grade: ⚪ — Arithmetic correct, but 4 pairs from 8 items is trivial**

**n=6 mapping**: 2 = phi(6). phi(6) = |{1,5}| = 2.

```

  Total AUs in FACS   = 44 (some sources say 46)

**Grade: ⚪ — The "12 core AUs" is not a standard number; cherry-picked**

Error rates: 8%, 13%, 21%, 17%, 36%, 36%.

  But: only 2 of 6 emotions hit this value.
  Average error = 21.8% ≈ GZ lower (0.212)?

```

```
  Error rate distribution (Fore people):

  36% |##################

**Average error ≈ GZ lower is interesting but post-hoc and sample-dependent.**

**Grade: 🟧 — Robust empirical value matching tau(6), but small number concern**

### H-PSY-007: Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve — 1/e at t=S

> Ebbinghaus (1885) forgetting curve: R(t) = e^(-t/S) where S = memory strength.
> At t = S: R = e^(-1) = 1/e ≈ 36.8%.
> The 1/e time constant is fundamental to memory decay.

**Psychology fact**: The exponential decay model R = e^(-t/S) is well-established
for forgetting. VERIFIED. (Some prefer power-law decay R = a*t^(-b), but
exponential is the standard textbook model.)

```
  R(t) = e^(-t/S)
  At t = S:  R = 1/e = 0.3679

  This is not unique to psychology — ALL exponential decays have 1/e.
  The claim is really: "memory decay is exponential" → 1/e appears.
  1/e appears in ANY exponential process.
```

**The 1/e connection is mathematically trivial — it's inherent in the exponential
function, not specific to the n=6 framework. Every exponential decay in physics,
biology, chemistry has a 1/e point.**

**Grade: ⚪ — Mathematically trivial (1/e in any exponential), not n=6 specific**

### H-PSY-008: Spacing Effect — Optimal Review Intervals

> Cepeda et al. (2006) found optimal spacing intervals for long-term retention.
> For a 1-day study session, optimal gap before test ≈ 1/3 of retention interval.
> 1/3 = meta fixed point of TECS-L model.

**Psychology fact**: Cepeda et al. (2006) meta-analysis found the optimal
inter-study interval is roughly 10-30% of the desired retention interval.
The "1/3 rule" is an approximation — actual optimal is closer to 10-20%.

```
  Cepeda et al. data (approximate):
  Retention interval | Optimal gap   | Ratio
  7 days             | 1 day         | 0.143
  35 days            | 8 days        | 0.229
  70 days            | 12 days       | 0.171
  350 days           | 21 days       | 0.060

  Average ratio ≈ 0.15, NOT 0.33
  The "1/3 rule" is a folk simplification, not supported by data.
```

**Grade: ⬛ — Factually wrong. Optimal spacing ratio ≈ 0.10-0.20, not 1/3**

  AB window: ~200-500ms after T1
  Simple RT:  150-300ms

  Proposed ratio: AB_duration / total_RT_range
  = 300ms / (700-150)ms = 300/550 = 0.545 ≈ 0.5 = GZ upper?

## E. Social Psychology & Networks (H-PSY-021 to H-PSY-025)

### H-PSY-021: Six Degrees of Separation = n

> Milgram (1967) small world experiment: average chain length ≈ 5.5-6.
> Watts & Strogatz (1998) confirmed in network models.
> 6 degrees of separation = n = perfect number.

**Psychology fact**: Milgram's original study found median chain length of 5.5-6.
Dodds et al. (2003) email replication found ~5-7 steps.
Facebook (2016) found average 3.57 degrees among all users.

```
  Milgram (1967):     ~5.5-6 steps
  Travers & Milgram:  5.2 median (completed chains only)
  Dodds et al (2003): ~5-7 (email)
  Facebook (2016):    3.57 (4.74 in 2011 study)

  The "6" in "six degrees" is approximate and has decreased with internet.
  Modern social networks: ~3.5-5 degrees.
```

**The iconic "6" is approximate and arguably outdated. The original Milgram study
had severe selection bias (only completed chains counted). Still, the cultural
resonance of "6 degrees" and the original ~5.5 result is notable.**

**Grade: 🟧 — Iconic result, approximately 6, but value is approximate and decreasing**

```

**The ratio 31/85 = 0.365 ≈ 1/e (0.3% error) is striking.

**Grade: 🟧 — 31/85 ≈ 1/e is numerically striking (0.3% error), but single study**
