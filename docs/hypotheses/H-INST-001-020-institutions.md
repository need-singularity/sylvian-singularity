## Background

Perfect number 6 has uniquely rich arithmetic properties:
- Divisors: 1, 2, 3, 6
- sigma(6) = 1+2+3+6 = 12 (sum of divisors)
- tau(6) = 4 (number of divisors)
- phi(6) = 2 (Euler totient)
- sigma_{-1}(6) = 1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/6 = 2 (reciprocal divisor sum = 2, unique to 6 among perfect numbers with this completeness property)

These numbers (2, 3, 4, 6, 12) pervade human civilization. The question is
whether this is because 6 is mathematically special, or because small numbers
are inherently common (Strong Law of Small Numbers).

## Verification Results

### Texas Sharpshooter Test

```
  Pool: 38 unique institutional numbers (jury sizes, org members, calendar,
        cycles, bases, trade units, small numbers 1-12)
  Method: Monte Carlo, 10,000 random numbers n=2..200

  n=6 matches:     11/38 institutional numbers
  Random average:   6.2 +/- 2.8
  n=6 Z-score:      1.54
  MC p-value:       0.088

  Matched items: {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 16, 24, 60, 144}
```

**p = 0.088 > 0.05. Does NOT pass Texas Sharpshooter at 5% level.**

The n=6 matches are above average (Z=1.54) but not statistically significant.
Many other numbers (n=4, 5, 11, 12, 15, 60) match equally well or better.

## Limitations

1. **Texas p=0.088 > 0.05**: Does not pass statistical significance threshold.
   n=6 is above average but not exceptional among integers 2-100.

2. **Strong Law of Small Numbers**: Most matches involve {2, 3, 4, 6, 12}.
   These appear everywhere regardless of n=6.

3. **No generalization**: n=28 (next perfect number) matches fewer facts.
   The pattern is specific to 6's cultural role, not perfect-number universality.

4. **Cultural confound**: Humans chose base-12/60 BECAUSE of 6's properties.
   The matches are circular: 6 caused the adoption, so finding 6 is expected.

5. **n=5 beats n=6**: In the Monte Carlo test, n=5 matches 14 institutional
   numbers vs n=6's 11. This undermines n=6 exceptionalism.
