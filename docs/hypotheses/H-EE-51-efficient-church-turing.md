# H-EE-51: Efficient Church-Turing Thesis via R=1

## Hypothesis

> Every physically realizable efficient computation can be simulated by an R=1 architecture with at most polynomial overhead. R=1 is to efficient computation what Turing machines are to computation in general. This is the "Efficient Church-Turing Thesis" grounded in n=6 thermodynamics.

## Background

- Church-Turing thesis: all computable functions are Turing-computable
- Extended CT: physical CT thesis says physics doesn't exceed Turing
- Efficient CT: polynomial-time computations are captured by specific models
- Proposal: R=1 architectures capture all polynomial-time computations at minimum energy
- R<1 architectures can compute the same things but waste energy (like reversible vs irreversible Turing machines)

## Predictions

1. Any BPP algorithm can be implemented on an R=1 architecture with O(1) energy overhead
2. R<1 architectures require Omega(1/R) energy overhead for the same computation
3. R=1 is the computational analog of reversible Turing machines (Bennett)
4. P != NP iff there exist problems that require R < 1/(polynomial) to solve

## Key Implications

- Computational complexity theory gains a physical (energy) dimension
- P vs NP may be equivalent to an energy efficiency question
- n=6 provides the bridge between abstract computation and physical implementation

## Conclusion

**Status:** Theoretical — requires formalization
**Destructiveness:** Maximum
**Bridge:** Computational complexity ↔ thermodynamics ↔ R(n)
