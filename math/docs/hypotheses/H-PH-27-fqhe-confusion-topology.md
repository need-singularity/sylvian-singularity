## Background / Context

### FQHE (Nobel Prize 1998, Tsui-Stormer-Laughlin)

The fractional quantum Hall effect occurs in 2D electron gases at low temperature
and high magnetic field. The Hall conductance is quantized at rational fractions
nu = p/q of e^2/h. These fractions form a hierarchy:

- nu = 1/3: Laughlin state (most robust, largest energy gap)
- nu = 2/5: Jain composite fermion state (next strongest)
- nu = 3/7: Higher-order composite fermion (weaker)
- nu = 5/2: Moore-Read state (non-Abelian anyons, most exotic)

The key physics: these plateaus are TOPOLOGICALLY PROTECTED. Small perturbations
(disorder, temperature fluctuations) do not destroy them. The quantization is
exact to parts per billion.

## Interpretation

The isomorphism between FQHE and confusion topology suggests that both phenomena
are instances of a deeper principle: n=6 arithmetic governs topologically
protected hierarchical structures, regardless of the physical substrate.

In FQHE:
- Anyons braid around each other in 2D space
- The braiding group determines the topological order
- Fractions are quantized by topology, not dynamics

In confusion topology:
- Semantic categories "braid" in embedding space
- The PH merge tree determines the confusion order
- Hierarchy is invariant to architecture, determined by data topology

The n=6 fractions are not imposed by the physicist or the ML engineer -- they
emerge from the topological structure of the system. This is the hallmark of
a universal principle.

The nu=5/2 / cross-domain parallel is particularly striking: both represent
states that break the simple hierarchical pattern. In FQHE, nu=5/2 hosts
non-Abelian anyons (useful for quantum computing). In confusion, cross-domain
pairs represent unexpected semantic connections (useful for creativity).

## Limitations

1. The mapping between FQHE and confusion is structural, not dynamical.
   FQHE operates in 2D electron gas at millikelvin temperatures and
   tesla-scale magnetic fields. Confusion operates in high-dimensional
   weight space. There is no known physical mechanism connecting them.

2. The FQHE fractions are small rationals (1/3, 2/5, 3/7, 5/2) that could
   match many arithmetic systems, not just n=6. The Strong Law of Small
   Numbers warns against over-interpreting such matches.

3. The confusion hierarchy has been tested on CIFAR-10 (10 classes) and
   a few other datasets. It has not been tested on datasets with hundreds
   or thousands of classes where richer hierarchical structure might emerge.

4. The "topological protection" in confusion (architecture-invariance) is
   empirical and has been tested on only 2-3 architectures. It could break
   for very different architectures (e.g., transformers vs CNNs).

5. The 4-level hierarchy may be an artifact of having exactly 4 FQHE
   fractions to match. More FQHE fractions exist (2/3, 4/9, etc.) and
   the mapping for those is not specified.
